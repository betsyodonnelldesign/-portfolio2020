Divvy Bikeshare Research & App Redesign

Who is the core audience using Divvy bikes? How can the mobile app be better suited to their needs?

PROJECT OVERVIEW

This was a one month long project as a student at Designation (now Flatiron School), a digital design school in Chicago. The team consisted of myself and three other design students. We contacted Divvy leadership and recieved support and excitement around the information we could deliver to improve the Divvy mobile app. This was conceptual work and was research as a deliverable, so it was not developed.

ROLE & TOOLS

UX-UI Design, User Research & Interviewing, Procreate, Photoshop, Illustrator

Understanding the Opportunity

Divvy users are prevalent throughout Chicago so it seemed like a natural fit for our team to choose this as our guerilla research project. Our goal was simple: Get outside and talk to real people using the divvy bikes and use structured research methods. The full Divvy experience consists of the website, kiosk, bikes, docks and mobile app.

01. Interviewing and Competitor Research

UX Research included: user interviews and observations, and competitor analysis. Some key insights found in research:

  • Confusion around pricing and resentment toward lack of pricing communication
  • Bike and docking station availability a major stressor in the experience
  • Time limit for using bikes is not as stressful as the inability to track the time limit
  • Concerns about the app draining battery life
  • Seek more information from out-of-town users and commuters being they are heavy users and with the most complaints

02. Personas and Studies

Using insights from interviewing real-life Divvy users, shown below, across a few heavy-use neighborhoods in Chicago, we created mirco-personas to document key traits and needs. We found a heavy swing toward tourists and daily summer commuters (given the time of year) plus the occasional user that uses the bikes for efficiently getting around town.

03. Journey Mapping

I focused on the commuter persona during the journey research process, to explore as many scenarios as possible. My other teammates focused on the other two personas and we worked as a unit to share collectively.

04. Mobile App Re-design

Taking everything we collected during research, my team and I prioritized enhancements to the existing mobile app in order of feasibility and importance. Below shows our modifications to the existing app:

  • Provide geographic information from the welcome screen such as: congestion, availability, and weather
  • Error prevention by using smart tracking of available bikes and docks, geolocation and destinations, and the option for the user to make reservations.
  • The largest UI element is the timer, using motion to ensure users are aware of their remaining time when the bike is claimed
  • Option within the interface to turn off GPS tracking to reserve battery life for power users so the user can still benefit from the timer

Below are more views of the timer function, using bold color as timer status indication. Using the app while biking is definitely not suggested, actually it is discouraged during app download. However testing assured us these features could be helpful and to keep a focus on sensory feedback.


For example, the ability for the user to reserve an open spot to dock their bike was a major enhancement to user feedback. However would need more testing to ensure the safety of this feature.

In addition to visuals, sensory feedback took a front seat in the design to provide the user important feedback while on their trip. We had to think in terms of the user biking vs. stopped/walking with the bike. Below shows a couple enhancements geared toward both cases:

  • Double-vibration and sound occurs when the timer is low to provide feedback while biking
  • Map with destination signifier as a convenience in-app vs. leaving to open an external application
  • Reservation details closest to touch area as most important for user during low timer period
